A **non-profit foundation** (or charitable foundation) is a type of tax-exempt organization that primarily functions to provide **funding and support** to other charitable organizations or causes, typically through **grants**.

Here is a breakdown of their core characteristics and the major distinctions:

---

## 🏛️ Core Characteristics of a Foundation

1.  **Grantmaking Focus:** Unlike a standard public charity (like a food bank or a university) which focuses on running programs and directly providing services, a foundation's primary function is often **grantmaking**—giving money to other non-profits to support *their* programs.
2.  **Funding Source:** Foundations are generally created by an initial, substantial **endowment**—a large initial contribution of money or assets—usually from a single source (an individual, a family, or a corporation).
3.  **No Owners/Shareholders:** Like all non-profits, a foundation has no owners or shareholders. Any income generated is invested back into the organization's charitable mission.
4.  **Tax-Exempt Status (e.g., 501(c)(3)):** In the US, most charitable foundations are recognized by the IRS as **501(c)(3)** organizations, meaning they are exempt from federal income tax, and contributions made to them are generally tax-deductible for the donor.

---

## 🎯 Major Types of Foundations (US Context)

The most critical distinction for legal and operational purposes is between **Private Foundations** and **Public Charities** (which sometimes include "public foundations" like community foundations).

| Feature | Private Foundation (Most Common Type) | Public Charity / Community Foundation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Funding Source** | Typically a **single source** (an individual, a family, or a corporation). | Receives broad financial support from the **general public**, government grants, and/or many different foundations. |
| **Oversight/Control** | Often controlled by the donor, their family, or representatives. | Governed by an independent board of directors. |
| **Function** | Primarily **makes grants** (non-operating) OR runs its own programs (operating). | Primarily **runs programs/provides services** (e.g., a hospital, university, museum). |
| **IRS Payout Rule** | Must annually distribute a minimum of **5%** of the fair market value of its assets for charitable purposes (for non-operating foundations). | No equivalent required annual payout rule. |
| **Tax Deduction for Donors** | Limited to lower percentages of the donor's adjusted gross income (AGI) than for Public Charities. | Donors can deduct a higher percentage of their AGI. |
| **Examples** | The Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, a small Family Foundation. | The Red Cross, a local community foundation, most universities. |

### Sub-Types of Private Foundations:

* **Private Non-Operating Foundation:** The most common type. Its sole or primary activity is making grants to other non-profit organizations. (e.g., The Ford Foundation).
* **Private Operating Foundation:** This type is actively involved in conducting its own charitable programs, such as running a museum, a research facility, or a retreat center, rather than just making grants to others.

---

## 📈 Role in Philanthropy

Foundations are the engine of much of modern philanthropy. By holding and investing large endowments, they provide a stable, long-term source of capital for the social sector. They can fund innovative or high-risk ventures that might not be suitable for individual public donors, providing the essential resources for other organizations to achieve their direct charitable missions.
